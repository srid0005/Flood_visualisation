Malaysia · Sixty Years of Flooding

A Nation Living
With Floods

Frequency, displacement and economic cost

Malaysia has recorded 74 significant flood events since 1965. The crisis is growing in frequency, scale, and economic damage — driven by the Northeast Monsoon and worsened by rapid urbanisation. This visualisation traces six decades of disaster using EM-DAT and DOSM survey data.

The Scale of the Crisis

Six decades of worsening flood records

Twelve Landmark Events That Defined the Crisis

Each bar = one major disaster. Length shows people displaced. Colour shows flood type. Hover for full details including deaths and month.

Key insight: The 1965 East Coast floods displaced 300,000 people. The 2014 Kelantan catastrophe and 2021 Selangor disaster are the modern equivalents. Displacement has not declined.

Displacement Surges Across Six Decades

Each dot = one recorded flood year. Line traces the trend. Drag the sliders to zoom into any time window — the chart rescales automatically.

Key insight: After 2000, multiple large events per year became the new normal. The post-2000 era is a new and more dangerous phase.

Which States Bore the Greatest Burden — and Did It Change?

Each coloured line = one state across two eras. A rising line means growing displacement. Use the dropdown to highlight a specific state.

Key insight: Pahang and Johor dominated 2000–2014. Sabah and Perlis are the rising states — their burden has grown sharply since 2015.

Patterns in 74 Disasters

When floods strike — and how severe they are

Every Flood Event — Seasonal Calendar

Each segment = one month. Inner ring = number of events. Outer ring = total people displaced. Colour = season. Hover any segment for exact figures.

Key insight: December alone has 21 recorded events and over 1.3 million displaced. The Northeast Monsoon (blue) dominates — it is the defining season of Malaysia's flood crisis.

State–Month Heatmap: Where and When Floods Cluster

Darker blue = more events in that state-month combination across 60 years. Numbers show event counts in the darkest cells. Click a state row to highlight it.

Key insight: Kelantan, Pahang, and Terengganu each have 10 events in December alone. Sabah's pattern is different — it peaks in the SW Monsoon months.

Are Deaths and Events Connected? A Diverging View

Each year: red bars left = deaths, blue bars right = events, centred at zero. Years with many events do not always have high death tolls.

Key insight: 2021 has the longest red bar (79 deaths) with only 8 events. 2016 had 4 events and zero deaths. Frequency does not predict lethality.

Geography

Where floods hit hardest across Malaysia

Flood Event Frequency by State

Darker blue = more recorded flood events since 1965. Johor, Pahang, Kelantan and Terengganu each have 18–19 events — near-annual flood exposure. Hover any state for details.

Key insight: East Coast peninsular states face near-annual flooding. This is not a rare disaster — it is a routine one.

Frequency vs. Severity — Four Risk Profiles

X-axis = event count. Y-axis = avg displaced per event. Bubble size = total displaced. Colour = region. Filter by region using dropdown.

Key insight: Perlis sits in "Rare but Catastrophic." Johor and Pahang are in "High Risk." Different states need completely different response strategies.

Human Cost

Deaths, displacement and six decades of loss

Total Deaths by Decade — The 2020s Are the Worst

Each lollipop = total flood deaths in that decade. Red = exceeds 90 deaths. Labels show exact figures. The 2020s already exceed all previous decades.

Key insight: The 2020s have recorded 108 deaths with half the decade remaining. Modern early-warning systems reduce per-event deaths — but more events means more total deaths.

High Displacement Does Not Always Mean High Deaths

Each panel = one decade. X = people displaced. Y = deaths. Drag slider to highlight deadly years.

Key insight: 2014 displaced 230,000 but killed only 17. 1967 killed 50 with fewer displaced. Displacement and lethality have decoupled since the 1990s.

Economic Toll

The financial burden of six decades of flooding

Most States Got Worse Between 2022 and 2024

Grey dot = 2022 loss. Coloured dot = 2024 loss. Red line = worsened. Teal = improved. Filter by direction.

Key insight: The majority of Malaysian states are on a worsening trajectory. Flood damage is not declining as a share of state economies.

What Gets Damaged Differs by State

Each bar = percentage of total damage by category. Toggle between % and RM view. Highlight a category using dropdown.

Key insight: Kelantan damage is 53% residential. Kedah is 69% other assets. A single national response cannot serve all states equally.

2021 Was a Once-in-a-Generation Shock

Filled area = annual flood damage in RM million. Red dashed line = pre-crisis average of RM 600M. 2021 cost RM 6.1 billion — ten times the average.

Key insight: Even in non-disaster years, Malaysia spends hundreds of millions recovering from floods. This is a permanent and growing fiscal burden.
Frequency

Flood events doubled after 2000. Eight events in 2021 alone — the highest ever recorded in a single year.

Geography

East Coast states bear disproportionate burden. Sabah and Perlis are the fastest-rising risk states.

Seasonality

December accounts for 1.3 million displaced across 60 years. The NE Monsoon is the defining crisis season.

Economics

2021 cost RM 6.1 billion — 0.4% of GDP. Damage is rising again in 2024 at RM 933M.
